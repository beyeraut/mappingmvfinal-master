PUBLIC ARCHAEOLOGY

Public archaeology is the sharing of archaeological information with people outside of archaeology, whether that knowledge is passed along through accessible information or through in-person experiences. During excavations at Morton Village, we welcome the public to stop by the site to see what we are working on. It is vital for the public to understand the importance of preserving the materials recovered and the site for future generations.

Left: Dr. Michael Conner discusses excavation procedures with the 2015 Taste of Archaeology participants. Right: Dr. Michael Conner discusses excavation procedures with participants in the 2013 SIUE field school.

In 2013, a public archaeology camp was created to show the importance of archaeology and Illinois’ cultural heritage. The camp was named “A Taste of Archaeology”. This program is a 4-day long archaeological experience, with no prior archaeological experience required. Attendees get hands-on experience excavating, learning proper methods as well as laboratory practices. In addition, the participants also learn about Illinois’ cultural heritage through an archaeological lens. This camp provides an in-person experience with archaeology; allowing participants to work alongside real archaeologists to become advocates for archaeology.

One benefit of an in-person archaeological experience is that we can educate participants about the importance of archaeological investigation and how to do it properly and legally. Often visitors to the site will tell us how they have removed artifacts from locations that they did not realize were archaeologically significant. One of our goals is educating participants in the camp and visitors to the site about the importance of leaving artifacts where you find them, as they could be disturbing significant archaeological contexts.
