EXCAVATION PROCEDURES

Archaeology is a destructive science, a time consuming and tedious process that focuses on recording all aspects of space modified by humans throughout time, including soil color and composition, location, type and amount of artifacts, and location of activities. During excavation, cultural deposits are identified as pit features based on a number of characteristics, including a roughly circular shape, darker color than the surrounding soil, and/or containing artifacts. A pit feature is a roughly circular pit that was used as for storage, cooking (i.e. a hearth), or trash disposal, and has been filled with soil and cultural materials.

Left: From left to right, Taste of Archaeology participant Hannah, MSU PhD Student Frank Raslich, and MSU Alumna Katie Soard use 1/4-inch mesh screens to sift dirt during the 2015 excavations. Right: Participants in the 2014 MSU archaeological field school begin 'clean-scraping' (excavation of a thin layer of dirt to reveal any disturbances in the natural soil) a 4-meter-by-4-meter square.

Another type of feature that we excavate are postmolds: small circular features that were created when holes were dug for posts. Over a long period of time, the original wooden posts left in the ground decompose and the soil becomes stained darker. When we excavate, it is presumed that as we dig through the multiple layers of earth, we are finding archaeological materials that were deposited in different contexts. Before excavating each of these layers, we record the upper elevations and draw and photograph the surface. We record all of the information about the features in field notebooks and on field forms throughout the excavation, which provides valuable information for future analyses and enables future researchers to check our work.
