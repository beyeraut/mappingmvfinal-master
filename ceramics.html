CERAMICS

Ceramics can tell you about many different aspects of prehistoric and historic societies. As ceramic vessels are primarily associated with cooking and serving food, they can give insights into the cooking practices and the foodways of the people being studied. Ceramics are also a flexible medium, allowing past potters to create a myriad of different forms and styles. These forms and styles, while occasionally serving a more functional purpose, can also reveal such things as social status, identity/ethnicity, and religion or ritual aspects.

Left: In this image, you can see in situ Oneota jar fragments found in Feature 159 during the 2009 excavations. Note on the far right sherd the horizontal and vertical trailing designs, which are characteristic of Oneota pottery. Right: MSU PhD Student Kate Frederick holds an Oneota jar shoulder sherd with a complicated design of trailed lines and punctates found during the 2012 field school excavations.

At Morton Village, we use the designs on ceramics to differentiate between Mississippian and Oneota contexts, as well as the interaction between the two. Another avenue of ongoing ceramic research at Morton Village is on use-wear and residue analysis. Looking at the carbonization patterns left by food charred during cooking, and the chemicals present within them, can inform us about the foodways at Morton Village and its possible role in social negotiation between the groups.

In this image, you can see a large segment of an Oneota jar, decorated with a zigzag trailed and punctate band and vertical trailing below. This design pattern is one of the most common Oneota patterns found at Morton village.
