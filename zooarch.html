ZOOARCHAEOLOGY

Zooarchaeology is the study of animal skeletal material from archaeological sites. One of the main goals studying of faunal (animal) bone is to determine the subsistence strategies used by the inhabitants of the site, i.e. what were they eating, where were they procuring their food resources.

Morton Village Faunal Remains

Faunal remains are present throughout the Morton Village site, however, Feature 224 represents a large feasting pit found within Structure 25 of the site. There are 44 animal taxa: 9 mammal species, 9 bird species, 5 reptile species, and 21 fish species; with over 3,000 individual identified skeletal specimens.

In these images, you can see some examples of faunal remains recovered at Morton Village.

Based on the analyzed animal remains, we know that the Morton Village people were exploiting diverse animal resources. The inhabitants of Morton Village were making deliberate food choices, from large mammals including white-tailed deer and wapiti (elk), down to small fish and reptiles such as sunfish and painted turtles.

Not only were animals consumed, their skeletal remains were utilized as tools. Throughout the site, human-modified animal bones have been uncovered including bone awls and pins. Looking at the modified skeletal elements, archaeologists are able to better understand the Morton Village inhabitants in terms of the how they created their tools and what those tools could be used for.
